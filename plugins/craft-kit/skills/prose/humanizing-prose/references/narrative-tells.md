# Narrative Tells & the Inverted Register

Sentence- and paragraph-level patterns to cut in a **narrative / article register**. The phrase-level cuts (throat-clearing, jargon, empty adverbs, meta-commentary) live in [phrases.md](./phrases.md) and the shared [scoring-rubric.md](../../shared/scoring-rubric.md); what is *specific* to this skill is the **inversion** below. Worked before/after in [examples.md](../assets/examples.md).

## The inverted register — what flips versus a technical doc

A technical pass (`tightening-prose`) keeps these because they carry density in a terse register. In narrative / article prose they are the loudest AI tells, so they go:

| Pattern | Technical doc | Narrative / article |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Em-dash (contrast or parenthetical) | keep — parenthetical density | **cut** — recast as a clean sentence |
| Short fragment ("No build. No tests.") | keep when intentional | **cut** — write the full sentence |
| Binary contrast ("not X, it's Y") | cut the rhetorical reflex | **cut** — even harder; it is the signature article tell |
| Precise adverb ("atomically", "lazily") | keep — changes meaning | rare in narrative; cut empty ones |
| Informative hedge ("likely", "approximately") | keep — marks uncertainty | keep when it carries real uncertainty |

Applying the *technical* carve-outs to an article is the main failure mode this skill exists to prevent: it leaves the em-dash contrasts and dramatic fragments that mark the text as machine-written.

## Structural anti-patterns to cut

- **Contrast / parenthetical em-dash** — the reflexive em-dash is the single loudest narrative AI tell. "What carries you isn't luck — it's consistency" → "Consistency carries you further than luck." Recast as a clean sentence or use a comma where one truly fits.
- **Binary-contrast crutch** — "It's not X, it's Y" / "isn't about A. It's about B" dodges the claim. State it: "Y." Cut it every time, not just when convenient.
- **Dramatic fragmentation** — "Not luck. Not connections. Consistency." → one sentence: "Consistency, not luck or connections, is what carries you."
- **Rhetorical reader-gambit** — a question posed only to answer it, or a "the question is whether you'll…" address to the reader. Make the assertion instead.
- **Negative listing for drama** — "Not A. Not B. C." → go straight to "C".
- **Staccato monotone** — a run of equally short punchy sentences reads as manufactured rhythm. Vary sentence length.
- **False agency** — "the decision emerges naturally", "the habit takes care of itself" → name who acts or why it follows.
- **Weak starters** — Wh-openers ("What if…") and a paragraph-leading "So" that announce instead of asserting. Lead with the subject or verb.
