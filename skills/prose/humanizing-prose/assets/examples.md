# Before / After Examples

Worked transformations to emulate, in the **narrative / article register**. The point of each is the *inverted carve-out*: the em-dash contrasts, binary-contrast crutches, and dramatic fragments that a technical pass would keep are cut here.

## 1 — Throat-clearing + binary contrast + fragments + rhetorical gambit

**Before:**

> Here's the thing: in today's fast-paced world, building a personal brand isn't just about posting content — it's about showing up authentically. Let me be clear: this is genuinely hard. Make no mistake, the stakes are high. Not luck. Not connections. Consistency. As we'll see, the reality is that most people give up too soon. So what if you could change that? Let that sink in.

**After:**

> A personal brand grows when you show up as yourself and keep doing it. That is hard, and most people stop before it pays off. Consistency carries you further than luck or connections ever will.

Cut: throat-clearing ("Here's the thing", "the reality is"), filler ("in today's fast-paced world"), the binary-contrast crutch and its em-dash ("isn't just about content — it's about…"), emphasis crutches ("Make no mistake", "Let that sink in"), the dramatic fragments ("Not luck. Not connections. Consistency."), and the rhetorical gambit ("So what if you could change that?"). Every claim is now stated straight.

## 2 — Filler + jargon + em-dash contrast + false agency

**Before:**

> When it comes to writing online, it's worth noting that consistency truly matters. At the end of the day, the implications are significant. Many creators leverage trends to grow — but the uncomfortable truth is that authenticity wins. Plot twist: the algorithm rewards those who show up. The decision to post daily emerges naturally once you understand this. Period.

**After:**

> Consistency is what makes writing online work. Trends bring quick growth, but writing in your own voice is what lasts. The algorithm rewards people who keep posting, so a daily habit pays off once you commit to it.

Cut: filler openers ("When it comes to", "it's worth noting", "At the end of the day"), empty adverb ("truly"), jargon ("leverage" → removed), vague declarative ("the implications are significant"), the em-dash contrast ("to grow — but the uncomfortable truth is…"), the performative "Plot twist:" and "Period.", and the false agency ("the decision emerges naturally" → named the cause). A plain "but" joining two real claims stays — the tell was the em-dash drama, not every contrast.

## 3 — When to STOP: this is a technical doc, not an article

**Before (do NOT run this skill on it):**

> The read-modify-write must be atomic — two concurrent requests for the same key cannot both spend the last token. Refill is computed lazily. No background ticker, no sweep over dormant buckets.

The em-dash is parenthetical, "atomically"/"lazily" carry technical meaning, and the fragment is deliberate density. These are correct in a terse technical register. Humanizing them would damage the text — this is `tightening-prose`'s territory, not this skill's. If the draft reads like a spec or README, switch passes.
